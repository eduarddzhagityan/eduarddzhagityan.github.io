Teaching

Teaching Philosophy

My teaching philosophy is grounded in the conviction that finance education should integrate theoretical rigor, institutional understanding, and practical experience. Students should not only learn how financial systems operate, but also understand why they may fail, how risks emerge, and how regulatory frameworks evolve in response to changing economic and financial conditions to secure the stability and resilience of financial institutions.

Drawing on both academic research and more than twenty-five years of professional experience in banking, I seek to connect conceptual frameworks with real-world financial practice. My courses link international business, financial markets, institutional frameworks, and global economic transformation; they combine analytical foundations, institutional perspectives, empirical evidence, and practical applications, encouraging students to develop critical thinking skills and a deeper understanding of modern financial systems.

My approach integrates lectures, case-based discussion, applied assignments, financial sector examples, and analytical frameworks. The objective is to help students move beyond memorization and develop the ability to interpret financial developments, evaluate institutional and system-wide risks, and apply financial concepts to real-world challenges.

Syllabi and Their Structure

International Finance

International financial markets; exchange rates; balance of payments; international monetary systems; capital flows; international trade finance; international credit; international financial institutions; financial risks.

Banking Systems and Central Banking

Bank as a financial institution; financial intermediation; transformation of banks and banking systems; banking mergers and acquisitions (M&A); banking risks; central banking; international banking regulation and supervision.

Financial Stability and Risk

Financial crises; systemic risk; macroprudential regulation; institutional resilience; financial sector governance.

International Financial Regulation

Basel I–III; regulatory structure in selected countries and regions; regulatory institutions, global and country-specific; regulatory models; micro- and macroprudential regulation; SIFIs, including G-SIBs and D-SIBs; resolution plans and living wills; Basel IV.

Research Methods in Economics

Undergraduate research papers; specifics of research in international economics; methods and structure of research; research sources; databases and scientometric indicators of academic publications.

Student Supervision and Mentoring

Throughout my academic career, I have supervised undergraduate theses, graduate and Ph.D. dissertations, research assistants, and independent research projects. I view supervision not merely as academic guidance, but as an opportunity to help students develop their analytical thinking, research skills, and professional confidence.

Many former students have subsequently pursued careers in finance, banking, consulting, public policy, and academic research.
