Teaching

Teaching Philosophy

My teaching philosophy is grounded in the conviction that finance education should integrate theoretical rigor, institutional understanding, and professional practice. Students should not only learn how financial systems operate, but also understand why they may fail, how risks emerge, and how regulatory frameworks evolve in response to changing economic and financial conditions to promote financial stability and systemic resilience.

Drawing on both academic research and more than twenty-five years of professional experience in banking, I seek to connect conceptual frameworks with real-world financial practice. My courses link international business, financial markets, institutional frameworks, and global economic transformation; they combine analytical foundations, institutional perspectives, empirical evidence, and practical applications, encouraging students to develop critical thinking skills and a deeper understanding of modern financial systems.

My approach integrates lectures, case-based discussions, applied assignments, financial sector examples, and analytical frameworks. The objective is to help students move beyond rote memorization and develop the ability to interpret financial developments, evaluate institutional and system-wide risks, and apply financial concepts to real-world challenges.

Student Supervision and Mentoring

Throughout my academic career, I have supervised undergraduate theses, master's theses, doctoral dissertations, research assistants, and independent research projects. I view supervision not merely as academic guidance, but as an opportunity to help students develop their analytical thinking, research skills, and professional confidence.

Former undergraduate and graduate students have subsequently pursued careers in finance, banking, consulting, public policy, and academic research.
